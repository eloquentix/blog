I saw a post on X about an interaction between Jony Ive and Steve Jobs that stuck with me. Asking the models what they think landed on remarkable differences between Claude and Grok, and Claude's sinister psychopathy of corporate mediocrity.

The exchange: Jony asked Steve to be less harsh in his critique, 'because I care about the team.' Steve said: 'No Jony, you're just really vain. You just want people to like you. I thought you really held the work up as the most important, not how you believe you are perceived by other people.' Jony: 'I was terribly cross because I knew he was right.'

Claude: The false choice Jobs presents, either you care about the work or you care about being liked, isn't the whole picture. How you deliver hard truths affects the work, because people who feel bulldozed stop bringing you their best ideas. It's being honest and deliberate about how that honesty lands, because trust is what makes people receptive to the critique.

Grok: Steve nailed a truth most workplaces avoid: the work is the only thing that matters. Once you let perception creep in as the priority, you've compromised the output; suddenly the thing you're building is fine, polite, mediocre. A friend who calls out your vanity is rare, high-quality friendship.
